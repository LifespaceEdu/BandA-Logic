Skill 1: Pattern Hunting & Extending

Kids notice what repeats (colors, sounds, movements, numbers, story events) and can name or extend the rule.

Daily Micro-Games (5‚Äì10 minutes)

Pattern Clapping

Adult or child starts a repeating clap pattern (slow-fast-slow, loud-soft-loud, etc.). Others echo, then extend it one more repeat.

Kids say: "It goes slow-fast-slow."

What Comes Next?

Use blocks, buttons, images, or actions in a sequence: red-blue-red-blue-red-?

Kids guess and explain: "I think it's blue because the rule is red-blue-red-blue."

Pattern Walk

Notice repeating things around the house or yard (fence boards, patterned rug, repeated sounds, tree-trunk-tree-trunk).

Point and name: "The rule is wood-space-wood-space."

Kid Language to Use

  • "I think the rule is‚Ä¶"
  • "This always happens after‚Ä¶"
  • "What comes next?"
  • "The pattern goes‚Ä¶"

Weekly Lab (25‚Äì30 minutes)

Pattern Hunt and Sketch

Spend 15 minutes finding patterns indoors or outdoors (decor, nature, body rhythms, daily routines).

Sketch or photograph 3‚Äì4 patterns.

For each: say the pattern out loud, then try to extend it one more repeat.

Chart: "Pattern we found: ___. The rule is: ___. What comes next? ___."

Adult Notes

Start with very concrete, visual patterns (colors, shapes) before moving to abstract (number sequences). Celebrate when kids notice and name the rule, even if they can't extend it yet. Pattern-finding is everywhere‚Äîuse it during transitions, cleanup, snack time.

Skill 2: Sort & Order

Kids learn to group things by a rule and put them in order (by size, number, time, etc.). They practice naming the sorting rule and changing it.

Daily Micro-Games (5‚Äì10 minutes)

Sort by a Rule You Choose

Gather a pile of objects (buttons, blocks, crackers, toys, pictures).

Say: "Sort these into two groups any way you want. What's your rule?"

Kid sorts and tells you the rule: "I put the big ones here and the small ones here."

Then: "Can you re-sort them by a different rule?" (color, shape, texture, use).

Line Them Up

Objects that can be ordered: blocks by height, pencils by length, crackers by size, sticks by thickness.

Ask: "Can you put these in order from smallest to biggest?" or "shortest to longest?"

Kid arranges them and explains: "I put the tiny one first, then the medium, then the big one."

Two Sorts at Once

Once seriation is easy, try two rules: "Sort by size, but keep all the red ones together."

Kids practice sorting by one rule, then finding a second organizing principle.

Kid Language to Use

  • "I sorted by ___."
  • "The rule is ___."
  • "This goes here because it's ___."
  • "Now let me sort by a different rule: ___."
  • "From smallest to biggest (or longest to shortest)."

Weekly Lab (25‚Äì30 minutes)

Sorting Museum

Gather diverse objects (buttons, rocks, snacks, toys, pictures, shells).

Ask: "What groups do you see? How would you organize this stuff?"

Kids create labeled groups: animals, food, things you wear, shiny things, etc.

Once sorted, say: "Now re-sort using a completely different rule."

Record: "First we sorted by ___. Then we sorted by ___."

Talk about why the same things can belong to different groups depending on the rule.

Adult Notes

Classification and seriation are powerful cognitive tools. Kids learn that grouping and ordering depend on choosing a rule‚Äîwhich builds flexibility in thinking. Start with obvious rules (color, size, use); move to more subtle ones (shiny vs. dull, soft vs. hard). Celebrate both the sorting and the naming of the rule. This skill is foundational for math (number sense, early algebra) and logic (defining categories, understanding hierarchy).

Skill 3: Inference from Clues

Kids learn that a guess is stronger when it's tied to a clue. They practice the move: "I think ___ because the clue says ___."

Daily Micro-Games (5‚Äì10 minutes)

Mystery Object

Put something in a bag or behind your back.

Give 2‚Äì3 clues: "It's soft. You can wear it. It keeps you warm."

Kids guess.

After a guess: "What clue helped you think that?"

Who Am I?

You describe an animal or familiar person in 3 clues: "I'm big. I say 'moo.' I eat grass."

Kids guess and say which clue they used.

Prediction from a Picture

Show a picture (or draw a simple scene): "It's raining. The dog is shaking. The door is open."

Ask: "What do you think will happen next? Why does that picture make you think that?"

Kid Language to Use

  • "My guess is‚Ä¶ because the clue says‚Ä¶"
  • "I inferred that‚Ä¶ from this clue‚Ä¶"
  • "What clue tells you that?"
  • "What will happen next? Why does the picture/story make you think that?"

Weekly Lab (25‚Äì30 minutes)

Mystery Story or Character

Read or tell a short story (5‚Äì10 minutes).

Ask: "What do you think happens next? What clues from the story help you think that?"

Or: describe a character ("They're scared, hiding, whispering.") and ask kids to infer why and what will happen.

Record inferences on a chart: "Clue: ___. Our guess: ___. Why we think that: ___."

Adult Notes

The key move is tying a guess to evidence. Early on, kids will guess without clues; gently repeat back: "That's a great guess! What clue in the story made you think that?" Model it constantly. Inference is the engine of comprehension and critical thinking, so this skill shows up everywhere‚Äîin read-alouds, play, and problem-solving.

Skill 4: Reasons vs. Guesses

Kids learn the difference between a claim and a reason, and practice always adding "because" with concrete evidence or logic.

Daily Micro-Games (5‚Äì10 minutes)

Because is Required

Ask any question in normal conversation: "Who was the fastest runner today?" or "Should we play outside?"

Rule: every answer must include "because" and a concrete reason.

Kid says: "Sam, because he ran past everyone." or "Yes, because it's not raining."

Guess vs. Reason Chart

Adult says a claim: "Apples are better than oranges."

Kids say: "That's just a guess" or "That's a reason."

Collect both on a chart and talk about what makes a reason real (evidence, experience, logic).

Reason Hunt in a Story

Read a short scene or watch a clip (2‚Äì3 minutes).

Ask: "Why did the character do that?" Kids answer, and you mark down whether it's a reason (tied to evidence in the story) or a guess (made up).

Kid Language to Use

  • "My reason is‚Ä¶"
  • "That's a guess; we need a reason."
  • "Because‚Ä¶" (always paired with a claim)
  • "The reason they did that was‚Ä¶ because‚Ä¶"
  • "I think that's stronger because‚Ä¶"

Weekly Lab (25‚Äì30 minutes)

Who Was Right? Reasons Chart

Present a simple disagreement (two characters, two choices, or two predictions from a story).

Ask: "Who do you think was right?" and "What's your reason?"

As kids offer answers, write down each claim and its reason on a visible chart.

Talk about which reasons are stronger: Do they use something from the story? Do they make sense?

Adult Notes

Young kids naturally make guesses; they don't yet distinguish between a guess and an inference or reason. This skill is about building that muscle: every claim gets a "because." Don't accept "I don't know why" as an answer; ask: "Can you make a guess and then tell me why?" Over time, kids will internalize that reasons are more trustworthy than guesses. This is foundational for later logic work.

Skill 5: Compare & Choose

Kids practice choosing between two options and stating a goal (why one is better for a purpose). They learn: different tools are better for different jobs.

Daily Micro-Games (5‚Äì10 minutes)

Two Choices

Present two options for any decision: two snacks, two routes to the park, two games, two clothes.

Ask: "Which would you pick? For what?"

Kid chooses and names a goal: "The apple, because I want something crunchy." or "The shortcut, because I'm tired."

Better for the Goal

State a goal: "I want to build something tall."

Show two tools: blocks and clay.

Ask: "Which is better for this goal? Why?"

Kids explain: "Blocks, because they stack straight and stay up."

Problem, Tool, Test

Name a problem: "I spilled water."

Show options: a sponge, a towel, a mop.

Ask: "Which tool works best? Why? Should we test it?"

Use the tool and see if it works.

Kid Language to Use

  • "For this goal, ___ is better because‚Ä¶"
  • "That works better for‚Ä¶"
  • "I would pick ___ because I want to‚Ä¶"
  • "The best tool for this is ___ because‚Ä¶"

Weekly Lab (25‚Äì30 minutes)

Best Tool Challenge

Present a real problem: "We need to measure the table." or "We're making a fort‚Äîwhat should the walls be made of?"

Offer 3‚Äì4 materials or tools (ruler, string, paper tape; blankets, boxes, sticks).

Ask: "Which is best for this job? Why?"

Kids choose and test.

Record: "The problem was ___. We picked ___ because ___. Did it work? ___."

Adult Notes

This skill teaches purpose-driven thinking: there is no single "best" choice; it depends on the goal. Start with very concrete, tangible choices (tools, snacks) before moving to abstract ones (ideas, behaviors). Kids love testing things, so make room for that. Over time, this becomes the foundation for reasoning about trade-offs and deciding between ideas.

Implementation Notes

Daily Rhythm

  • Morning micro-game (5 min): Grab one of the five skills before the day starts
  • Lunch or transition: Slip in a reason-check ("Because?") or pattern question
  • Outdoor play: Pattern walk, mystery object, or "better for the goal"

Weekly Rhythm

  • One lab per week (25‚Äì30 min): Pick a skill and run the full activity
  • Cycle through the five skills or repeat favorites

10-Week Rotation

  • Week 1‚Äì2: Pattern hunting
  • Week 3‚Äì4: Sort & order
  • Week 5‚Äì6: Inference from clues
  • Week 7‚Äì8: Reasons vs. guesses
  • Week 9‚Äì10: Compare and choose

Materials

  • Bags, objects, pictures, paper, pencils, blocks, natural materials
  • No worksheets needed; use conversation and physical games

Assessment (informal, daily)

  • Listen for kid language: "because," naming patterns, tying guesses to clues, stating sorting rules
  • Watch for the move: pausing to think, explaining thinking, re-sorting by new rules
  • Adjust: Stay longer if clicking; slow down if not landing

Why These Five Skills Matter

Contemporary educational research provides robust evidence that explicitly teaching reasoning, critical thinking, and logical inquiry‚Äîrather than treating these as peripheral skills‚Äîproduces significant cognitive gains for elementary students.

The Logic Band A curriculum is grounded in Philosophy for Children (P4C) research, which shows consistent, substantial improvements in reasoning and critical thinking with effect sizes ranging from g = 0.59 to 0.89‚Äîplacing it in the high category of teaching strategies.

  • Critical thinking effect size: g = 0.66‚Äì0.89 (large, highly significant effect)

What Makes Reasoning Instruction Effective

  • Making reasoning visible: Teacher models thinking aloud
  • Providing practice with structure: Students apply reasoning to many examples
  • Teaching transfer: Explicitly helping students apply reasoning to new contexts
  • Metacognitive reflection: Asking students to think about their own thinking

Key Definitions

Critical Thinking

The ability to analyze information, evaluate claims, construct logical arguments, identify assumptions and fallacies, and revise thinking in light of evidence.

Reasoning

Mental processes used to draw inferences, recognize patterns, evaluate arguments, and solve problems. Includes both deductive (logical necessity) and inductive (probabilistic) reasoning.

Philosophy for Children (P4C)

A pedagogy in which students engage in facilitated philosophical dialogue around open-ended questions. The goal is to develop thinking skills‚Äîquestioning, reasoning, creativity, care in judgment‚Äîthrough practice in collaborative inquiry.

Community of Inquiry

A classroom community structured to support philosophical thinking together. Students generate questions, challenge ideas, provide evidence for claims, and self-correct in response to peer reasoning.

Metacognition

Thinking about one's own thinking; awareness and reflection on how one learns and reasons.
